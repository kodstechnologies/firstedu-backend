## Passage 1 (Q1–Q5)

Urban biodiversity is often dismissed as a lesser concern than the preservation of untouched wilderness, yet cities harbour ecological relationships every bit as intricate as those found in remote forests. A single old banyan tree in a metropolitan park can support dozens of bird species, insect colonies, and epiphytic plants, forming a compressed but functioning ecosystem within a few hundred square metres. Municipal planners, however, have historically treated green cover as an aesthetic afterthought rather than infrastructure — something to be added once roads, drains, and buildings are finished, rather than designed alongside them.

This sequencing has consequences. When trees are planted as ornamental additions to already-hardened landscapes, they rarely form the interconnected canopy networks that allow species to move between patches; each tree becomes an ecological island rather than a node in a wider web. Studies of several Indian metropolitan areas have shown that fragmented green cover, even when substantial in total area, supports markedly less biodiversity than the same acreage arranged as connected corridors. The implication is uncomfortable for city planners: it is not merely the quantity of green space that matters, but its configuration.

Some cities have begun reversing this logic by mandating "green corridors" — continuous strips of vegetation along roads, canals, and rail lines — specifically to reconnect fragmented habitats. Critics argue this is a cosmetic response to a structural problem, since corridors cannot compensate for the outright loss of large habitat patches to construction. Proponents counter that corridors are not a substitute for large green spaces but a necessary complement, particularly in already-dense cities where reclaiming large tracts of land is not politically or economically feasible.

(D) Green corridors are a complete solution to urban biodiversity loss
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** The passage's turning point ("it is not merely the quantity of green space that matters, but its configuration") is the thesis. (A) overreaches. (C) mistakes an illustrative example for the main claim. (D) contradicts the passage, which presents corridors as a "complement," not a complete solution.

**EXPLANATION:** The sentence contrasts "ecological island" with being "a node in a wider web," signalling isolation. (A) and (D) misread "island" literally; (B) confuses it with a geographic meaning.

**Q3.** Based on the passage, which of the following would the author most likely support?

## Passage 2 (Q6–Q10)

The handwritten letter has become, in the span of a single generation, an object of nostalgia rather than necessity. Where a personal letter once required its writer to commit thought to paper in a single, largely irreversible pass — crossing out errors rather than deleting them, choosing words with the knowledge that revision was costly — digital messages invite a different relationship with language altogether. A text message can be typed, deleted, retyped, and sent in seconds, with none of the friction that once forced deliberation.

It would be easy to conclude from this that digital communication has simply made writing faster without changing its character. But something subtler appears to be happening. Because digital messages are cheap to send and expected to arrive instantly, they have shifted toward brevity and immediacy at the expense of the sustained, developed thought that slower correspondence once encouraged. A letter, written with the knowledge that a reply might take weeks, tended to accumulate the writer's reflections over that interval; a text message, written with the expectation of an answer within minutes, rarely affords the same interior monologue.

None of this is to say that digital communication is impoverished relative to letters in every respect — it enables forms of connection, such as constant low-stakes contact across long distances, that letter-writing never could sustain. But it is worth noticing that ease of transmission and depth of reflection may trade off against each other, and that the loss of friction in how we write has quietly reshaped what we choose to say.

**EXPLANATION:** (A) overstates — contradicted by the final acknowledgment of digital's advantages. (C) is a recommendation never made. (D) reverses attribution: constant low-stakes contact is credited to digital communication, not letters.

This shift complicates a simple narrative in which renewable energy adoption is understood purely as an environmental or moral choice made at some economic cost. Solar and wind are intermittent — dependent on weather and time of day — in a way that coal and gas plants are not, and integrating large shares of intermittent power into a stable electricity grid requires additional investment in storage and transmission infrastructure that is not always reflected in headline generation-cost figures. Critics of overly optimistic renewable-cost narratives argue that ignoring these integration costs paints an incomplete picture of the true price of a renewables-heavy grid.

## Passage 4 (Q16–Q20)

Satire occupies an uneasy position among literary forms: it depends for its effect on being taken seriously enough to sting, while simultaneously being recognised as not literally meant. A satirical proposal — however outrageous on its surface — only succeeds if the audience understands both that it is not a genuine policy suggestion and that it nonetheless points at something true about the folly or cruelty it exaggerates. When either half of this understanding fails, satire collapses: taken too literally, it is mistaken for a sincere and often repugnant proposal; recognised as mere joke without the underlying critique landing, it becomes empty absurdity, amusing perhaps, but toothless.

This dual requirement explains why satire ages unevenly. A satirical work aimed at a specific, well-known target — a particular politician's rhetoric, a specific policy debate — can lose its force once its audience no longer recognises the target, even if the underlying human folly it critiques (vanity, hypocrisy, cruelty dressed as virtue) remains timeless. Readers centuries removed from the original controversy may need extensive footnotes to recover the joke's referent, by which point much of its original sting has necessarily dulled.

This is not a flaw unique to satire; all topical humor depends on shared context between writer and audience. But satire's dependence on a precise, dual recognition — the surface absurdity and the buried truth — makes the form especially fragile across time, even as its best examples continue to be read for other reasons: their prose style, their construction, or the historical window they offer onto the concerns of their age.

**Q16.** According to the passage, what are the two conditions necessary for satire to succeed?
(A) The audience must find it funny and the author must be well-known
(B) The audience must recognise it is not a literal proposal, and must also grasp the underlying truth it points to
(C) The work must be published in a respected journal and reviewed positively
(D) The satire must target only political figures
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** Directly stated as the "dual requirement." (A), (C), (D) are not stated conditions.

**Q17.** Why, according to the passage, does topical satire tend to lose force over time?
(A) Because language itself becomes harder to understand with age
(B) Because audiences centuries later may no longer recognise the specific target being satirised, even if the underlying human folly remains relevant
(C) Because printing technology has changed
(D) Because satire is inherently a lesser literary form
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** Supported by the discussion of needing "extensive footnotes to recover the joke's referent." (A), (C), (D) are unsupported.

**Q18.** The passage suggests that satire which is "recognised as mere joke without the underlying critique landing" becomes:
(A) More effective than intended
(B) Empty absurdity that is amusing but lacks force
(C) Indistinguishable from a sincere proposal
(D) Immediately forgotten by all readers
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** Directly quoted ("empty absurdity, amusing perhaps, but toothless"). (A) is the opposite. (C) describes the other failure mode. (D) is unsupported.

**Q19.** What is the relationship between satire and general topical humor, according to the final paragraph?
(A) Satire is entirely unrelated to topical humor
(B) Satire shares the general fragility of topical humor across time, but is especially fragile due to its additional dual-recognition requirement

(D) Topical humor is a subset of satire
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** Directly supported ("not a flaw unique to satire... makes the form especially fragile"). (A), (C), (D) misstate this relationship.

**Q20.** Which of the following examples would the passage's argument best predict to be true?
(A) A satirical essay mocking a now-obscure 18th-century tax policy is, today, primarily studied for its prose style rather than for its original comic sting
(B) A satirical essay about a timeless human folly will always be immediately understood by all future readers without any context

(D) Satire targeting well-known figures is always funnier than satire targeting policies
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** Matches the passage's prediction for topical satire whose target has faded ("continue to be read for other reasons: their prose style"). (B) contradicts the need for shared context. (C) and (D) overstate claims the passage never makes.

## Passage 5 (Q21–Q24)

The shift toward remote work, accelerated by necessity rather than by design, has produced a body of evidence that resists a simple verdict. Early surveys suggested employees were, by self-report, more productive working from home — free of commute time and office interruptions. Yet more granular studies, tracking actual output rather than self-reported perception, have painted a more mixed picture: individual, focused tasks often benefit from the quiet of home, while collaborative, creative work that depends on spontaneous exchange between colleagues appears to suffer measurably when conducted entirely through scheduled video calls.

The distinction matters for how organisations should think about remote work, because it suggests the debate has often been framed at the wrong level of granularity. Asking whether remote work is "more productive" than office work treats productivity as a single, uniform quantity, when the evidence suggests its effects vary by task type. A software engineer debugging code alone may lose nothing, and gain considerable focus, by working from home; a product team attempting to generate a genuinely novel idea through collaborative brainstorming may lose something that scheduled video calls, however well-run, do not fully replace.

This suggests that the most useful organisational question is not "remote or office?" but rather which specific activities benefit from which specific arrangement — a more demanding question to answer, but one considerably closer to what the evidence actually supports.

**Q21.** What is the main argument of the passage?
(A) Remote work is unambiguously more productive than office work
(B) The productivity effects of remote work vary by task type, making a single verdict on remote work as a whole misleading
(C) Office work should be abolished entirely
(D) Self-reported productivity surveys are always reliable
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** Directly stated as the passage's core claim. (A) and (C) overstate. (D) is contradicted by the passage's preference for granular studies over self-report.

**Q22.** According to the passage, what type of work tends to suffer when conducted entirely remotely?
(A) Individual, focused tasks
(B) Collaborative, creative work depending on spontaneous exchange
(C) Any task done using a computer
(D) Tasks performed by senior employees only
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** Directly stated. (A) is the opposite (individual tasks tend to benefit). (C), (D) are unsupported.

**Q23.** The passage suggests that early surveys showing higher self-reported productivity were:
(A) Completely fabricated
(B) Potentially incomplete, since more granular studies revealed a more mixed, task-dependent picture
(C) The final and most reliable word on the subject
(D) Focused exclusively on collaborative work
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** The passage frames early surveys as an initial finding later complicated by granular research. (A) overstates. (C) contradicts the passage's preference for later findings. (D) is inaccurate.

**Q24.** What does the author ultimately recommend organisations focus on?
(A) Whether to be remote or office-based as a single blanket policy
(B) Determining which specific activities benefit from which specific work arrangement
(C) Eliminating collaborative work entirely
(D) Relying solely on employee self-reports of productivity

**EXPLANATION:** Directly stated in the final sentence. (A) is what the author argues against. (C), (D) are unsupported.

## Set 1 (Q53–Q57)

**Principle:** A person who voluntarily consents to the risk of harm inherent in an activity cannot later claim damages for injury arising from that inherent risk (*volenti non fit injuria*). **Q53.** Will Ravi's claim succeed?

(B) No, because rock climbing is inherently dangerous
(C) Yes, because the injury arose from the instructor's negligence in failing to inspect the rope, not from an inherent risk Ravi consented to
(D) Yes, because waivers are never legally valid
**ANSWER: C**
**EXPLANATION:** *Volenti non fit injuria* does not shield a defendant from harm caused by negligence beyond the activity's ordinary risks. (A), (B) misapply the defense to negligence-caused harm. (D) is an overstatement unsupported by the principle, which only limits waivers in specific circumstances.

(C) No, because manufacturing flaws are always the company's responsibility
(D) Yes, because Ravi never signed a valid waiver
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** If the flaw was undetectable despite reasonable inspection, there is no negligence "beyond the ordinary risk." (A), (C) ignore the negligence carve-out. (D) is factually inconsistent with the facts.

**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** The principle explicitly excludes consent obtained "under duress." (A), (C) ignore this carve-out. (D) states a timing rule not found in the passage.

## Set 3 (Q91–Q95)

A city library reports that in-person visits have declined by 40% over the past five years, while its budget has remained flat. The library director proposes closing two of the library's five neighborhood branches to consolidate resources into the three busiest locations, arguing this will improve service quality at the remaining branches without requiring additional funding.

**Q91.** Which of the following, if true, would most strongly support the director's proposal?
(A) The two branches proposed for closure have seen the sharpest declines in visits and are located within walking distance of one of the three remaining branches
(B) The library's total book collection has grown in the past five years
(C) The city's population has grown by 10% over the past five years
(D) Library staff have received positive performance reviews
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** Directly supports the specific rationale — targeting low-use branches whose patrons have a viable nearby alternative. (B), (C), (D) do not address whether closing specifically those two branches serves patrons well.

**Q92.** Which of the following, if true, would most undermine the director's proposal?
(A) One of the two branches proposed for closure is the only library location accessible by public transit for a large, low-income neighborhood with no nearby alternative
(B) The remaining three branches have slightly larger parking lots
(C) The library board has approved budget increases in the past
(D) Total visits across the entire city have declined, not just at these two branches
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** Identifies that closing this specific branch would eliminate access for an underserved population with no alternative, undermining the "without harming service" claim. (B), (C) are irrelevant. (D) somewhat supports the general consolidation logic rather than undermining it.

**Q93.** What assumption is the director making in proposing that consolidation will "improve service quality... without requiring additional funding"?

(D) That online library services can fully replace in-person visits
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** This is the load-bearing assumption connecting consolidation to improved service without harming access. (B), (C), (D) are not necessary for this specific logical claim.

**Q94.** Which of the following would be the most relevant piece of additional information needed to evaluate the proposal fairly?

(B) Usage patterns and demographic reliance at each of the five branches individually
(C) The color scheme of the proposed renovated branches
(D) The library director's personal reading preferences
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** Directly relevant to assessing which branches can be closed with least harm. (A), (C), (D) are irrelevant.

**Q95.** If the two branches proposed for closure together account for only 8% of total system-wide visits, while requiring 30% of the total budget to operate, how does this affect the strength of the director's proposal?

(D) It proves the director acted in bad faith
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** Directly supports the proposal's stated logic — freeing disproportionate cost from low-use branches for reallocation. (A) misreads the significance of low usage. (C) ignores clear relevance. (D) is an unsupported motive claim.

**Q96.** Which of the following, if true, would most weaken the manufacturer's claim?

**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** The gold-standard controlled comparison with objective measures directly addresses both flaws critics identified. (B), (C), (D) are irrelevant to causal validity.

**Q98.** What are the two specific methodological criticisms raised against the original survey?

## Set 5 (Q101–Q104)

A city installed automated speed cameras at ten high-accident intersections. Over the following year, recorded accidents at those ten intersections fell by 30%. City officials cite this as proof that the cameras deterred speeding and thus prevented accidents.

**Q101.** Which of the following, if true, would most weaken the officials' conclusion?
(A) During the same year, accidents fell by 28% citywide, including at intersections without any cameras, due to a broader road-safety education campaign launched that year
(B) The cameras cost ₹2 crore to install
(C) Drivers dislike being fined by automated systems
(D) The ten intersections were chosen based on historical accident data
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** A similar citywide decline independent of the cameras suggests another cause may explain most of the drop. (B), (C), (D) don't address the causal attribution question.

**Q102.** Which of the following, if true, would most strengthen the officials' conclusion?
(A) Accidents at the ten camera intersections fell by 30%, while accidents at comparable, similarly-selected intersections without cameras (matched for prior accident rates) remained essentially unchanged over the same year
(B) The cameras take a photo of every vehicle's license plate
(C) The city government is popular among voters
(D) The cameras were installed by a well-known contractor
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** A proper matched-comparison showing no change at similar non-camera intersections isolates the camera's effect. (B), (C), (D) are irrelevant to causal attribution.

**Q103.** What is the implicit assumption in the officials' original reasoning?
(A) That the recorded accident decline at these ten intersections was not primarily driven by factors unrelated to the cameras
(B) That all drivers are aware of the exact location of each camera
(C) That accidents recorded at other intersections do not matter
(D) That speed cameras are cheaper than hiring traffic police
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** This is the necessary unstated assumption underlying the causal leap. (B), (C), (D) are not required assumptions.

**Q104.** The reasoning flaw in the officials' conclusion is most similar to which of the following?
(A) A city plants more trees in a park and, after birds are seen nesting there the same year, concludes the trees caused increased city-wide bird migration, without checking whether bird populations rose elsewhere too
(B) A city bans a chemical known through repeated controlled trials to cause water contamination, and contamination levels subsequently fall
(C) A hospital introduces a new surgical protocol, tests it in a randomized trial against the old protocol, and finds significantly better outcomes
(D) A school raises math scores after, in a controlled study, comparing classes randomly assigned to a new teaching method versus classes using the old method
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** Structurally identical flaw: observing a change coinciding with an intervention, without checking whether the same change occurred independently elsewhere. (B), (C), (D) all involve actual controlled/experimental verification — a structurally different situation.

## Set 1 (Q105–Q109)

**Data:** A company's quarterly revenue (in ₹ lakh) for four quarters of a year was: Q1 = 120, Q2 = 150, Q3 = 135, Q4 = 195. Company expenses were a constant 60% of revenue in each quarter.

**Q105.** What was the company's total profit (revenue − expenses) for the full year?
(A) ₹240 lakh
(B) ₹200 lakh
(C) ₹216 lakh
(D) ₹180 lakh
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** Total revenue = 120+150+135+195 = ₹600 lakh. Total expenses = 60% × 600 = ₹360 lakh. Profit = 600 − 360 = ₹240 lakh. (B), (C), (D) result from arithmetic errors in summing revenue or applying the percentage.

**Q106.** By what percentage did revenue grow from Q1 to Q4?
(A) 50%
(B) 62.5%
(C) 60%
(D) 75%
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** Growth = (195 − 120)/120 × 100 = 75/120 × 100 = 62.5%. (A), (C), (D) are miscalculations of the same formula.

**Q107.** What was the average quarterly revenue for the year?
(A) ₹140 lakh
(B) ₹150 lakh
(C) ₹135 lakh
(D) ₹160 lakh
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** Average = 600 / 4 = ₹150 lakh. Other options do not correctly divide the verified total by 4.

**Q108.** In which quarter was the profit (in absolute ₹) the highest?
(A) Q1
(B) Q2
(C) Q3
(D) Q4
**ANSWER: D**
**EXPLANATION:** Since expenses are a constant 60% of revenue every quarter, profit (40% of revenue) is directly proportional to revenue. Q4 has the highest revenue (₹195 lakh) and therefore the highest profit (40% × 195 = ₹78 lakh).

**Q109.** What was the ratio of Q2 revenue to Q3 revenue, in simplest form?
(A) 10:9
(B) 9:10
(C) 5:4
(D) 4:5
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** Q2:Q3 = 150:135. Dividing both by their GCD (15) gives 10:9. (B) inverts the ratio. (C), (D) do not match the actual values.

**Data:** A school has 800 students. 45% of the students are girls, and the remainder are boys. Among the girls, 60% are enrolled in the science stream. Among the boys, 50% are enrolled in the science stream.

**Q110.** How many girls are enrolled in the science stream?
(A) 210
(B) 216
(C) 220
(D) 200
**ANSWER: B**

**Q111.** What is the total number of students (boys + girls) enrolled in the science stream?

(B) 436
(C) 440
(D) 420
**ANSWER: B**
**EXPLANATION:** Boys = 800 − 360 = 440. Boys in science = 50% × 440 = 220. Total in science = 216 (girls) + 220 (boys) = 436.

**Q112.** What percentage of the total student body (800) is enrolled in the science stream?
(A) 50%
(B) 54.5%
(C) 55%
(D) 60%

**Q113.** What is the ratio of boys to girls within the science stream, in simplest form?
(A) 55:54
(B) 54:55
(C) 5:4
(D) 10:9

**Q114.** If 10% of the boys currently in the science stream drop out, how many boys would remain in the science stream?

## Set 3 — Profit, Loss & Simple Interest (Q116–Q120)

**Q116.** A shopkeeper buys an item for ₹800 and sells it for ₹960. Find the profit percentage.
(A) 20%
(B) 15%
(C) 25%
(D) 18%
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** Profit = 960 − 800 = ₹160. Profit % = 160/800 × 100 = 20%.

**Q117.** A sum of ₹5,000 is invested at simple interest at a rate of 8% per annum for 3 years. Find the interest earned.
(A) ₹1,200
(B) ₹1,000
(C) ₹1,500
(D) ₹1,350
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** SI = P×R×T/100 = 5000×8×3/100 = ₹1,200.

**Q118.** What will be the total amount (principal + interest) after those 3 years (continuing from Q117)?
(A) ₹6,200
(B) ₹6,000
(C) ₹6,500
(D) ₹6,100
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** Amount = Principal + Interest = 5,000 + 1,200 = ₹6,200.

**Q119.** A trader marks up goods by 25% above cost price, then gives a discount of 10% on the marked price. Find the overall profit percentage.
(A) 12.5%
(B) 15%
(C) 10%
(D) 20%
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** Assume cost price = ₹100. Marked price = 100 × 1.25 = ₹125. Discount = 10% × 125 = ₹12.5. Selling price = 125 − 12.5 = ₹112.5. Profit = ₹12.5 on a cost of ₹100 = 12.5% profit.

**Q120.** If the simple interest on a sum for 2 years at 10% per annum is ₹400, find the sum.
(A) ₹2,000
(B) ₹1,800
(C) ₹2,200
(D) ₹2,500
**ANSWER: A**
**EXPLANATION:** SI = P×R×T/100 → 400 = P×10×2/100 = P×0.2 → P = 400/0.2 = ₹2,000.
