PASSAGE SOURCING RULES:
- Base each passage on real, verifiable subject matter (a legal principle, a current 
  event, an editorial argument, a logical puzzle) but WRITE IT ORIGINALLY — do not 
  copy or closely paraphrase any real article, judgment, or published text. 
  Adapt facts/concepts into your own prose.
- For Legal Reasoning: state the legal principle explicitly in the passage. Questions 
  must be answerable using ONLY the stated principle + fact pattern — no outside 
  legal knowledge required, unless the passage explicitly says "apply your knowledge 
  of X law."
- For Current Affairs: only use events/facts you are confident are real and dated 
  correctly. If uncertain of a specific fact, do not include it.

DIFFICULTY CALIBRATION (important — do NOT make everything maximally hard):
Within each passage's question set, distribute difficulty realistically:
- ~30% direct/inference-light (testable by careful reading, 1 elimination step)
- ~50% moderate (requires connecting 2 parts of passage, or eliminating 2 close 
  distractors)
- ~20% hard (requires precise scope-reading, negation-handling, or multi-step 
  elimination)
A paper that is uniformly "extremely hard" reads as artificial, not authentic — 
real CLAT has a difficulty gradient. Match that gradient, not a flat ceiling.

QUESTION CONSTRUCTION RULES:
- Write the question FIRST as a real comprehension/reasoning question. Do NOT invent 
  an answer and then reverse-engineer a passage/question to fit it — this produces 
  circular, gameable questions. The correct answer must fall out of applying the 
  stated facts/principle, not be assumed in advance.
- Exactly one option is correct. Verify this yourself before finalizing each question: 
  restate why each of the other 3 options fails, in one line, before moving on.
- Distractors must be plausible: same topic, same length/register as the correct 
  answer, and wrong for a specific, statable reason (not just "irrelevant").
- No option should be correct "partially" or "more correct than others" — CLAT-style 
  answers are unambiguous once the passage is read correctly.
- Avoid: negative-stacked phrasing ("which of the following is NOT NOT..."), options 
  that hinge on a single ambiguous word, and questions answerable without reading 
  the passage.
